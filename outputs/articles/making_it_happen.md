# Making It Happen vs Letting It Unfold

You are three days into waiting for someone to get back to you. The email was sent. The proposal is reasonable. By any honest measure, the ball is in their court.

But there is an itch. A pull to send a follow-up. To phrase it slightly differently. To pick up the phone instead. To do something — anything — that might shake the situation loose, because surely a little more push from you would help things along.

Sometimes that push is exactly what is needed.

Sometimes it is the thing that costs you the deal.

Most of us have a strong preference between making it happen and letting it unfold. We tend to trust one of these moves more than the other, and we use it without much thought — at work, in friendships, in our families, in the small choices about how we spend a Tuesday afternoon. We may not even notice we are choosing.

This is the polarity between agency and receptivity.

Making it happen is the energy of pursuit: deciding, buying the ticket, sending the email, picking up the phone, refusing to wait for life to come around to us. Without it, much of what matters in our lives would remain only an idea.

Letting it unfold is a different intelligence. It is not waiting around. It is not abdicating. It is the discipline of allowing other people, timing, and reality itself to participate in the outcome — and being willing to listen to what they say.

I have spent a lot of my life making things happen. I have moved to five different countries and back. One year I had to file taxes in four of them, which is exactly as glamorous as it sounds.

The last move was the big one. I had fallen in love with someone who lived in San Francisco, and I was living in Singapore. After enough 17-hour flights and a 15-hour time difference that we kept pretending was workable, I project-managed a transpacific move so we could finally see where the relationship would go. Naturally.

In the moment, I felt proud. Look at what I had organized. Look at the spreadsheets, the timing, the way I had not let my job, my apartment, or basic geography slow me down.

In hindsight, I notice something else.

I notice a person who was fluent in making things happen and far less fluent in letting things reveal themselves. I notice that my partner had not yet started his divorce at the time of my move — a fact that might not have mattered to everyone, but mattered to me. Letting that unfold first, instead of building around it, might have given me information about the shape and pace of what was actually possible. It might have changed when I moved, or whether I did. I will never know.

The point is not that I should have waited. The point is that I had one move, and I used it for everything.

## Making it happen, wisely used

Making it happen is powerful energy. It turns a vague desire into a real plan and a private commitment into something the world can see. It is the move of the person who decides to learn the language, leave the company, write the proposal, set the date, have the hard conversation. It refuses the slow drift of waiting for life to figure itself out.

At its best, making it happen is not control. It is responsibility in motion. It says: this matters to me, and I am willing to put my weight behind it. It is how love, courage, and care reach the part of the world where they actually have to land.

## Making it happen, overused

When making it happen becomes the only move we have, we start treating every obstacle as something to overcome, rather than as possible information. We stop receiving signals that do not fit our plan. We may carry conversations that ought to be jointly held, force timing that needed more breathing room, or shape circumstances so completely that other people never get the chance to show us what they would do on their own.

Over time, overused making-it-happen can become lonely. We may end up in a life we technically built but did not let anyone fully choose into alongside us. We may also exhaust ourselves, because once we are the one moving everything, there is no version of the situation in which we get to stop.

## Letting it unfold, wisely used

Letting it unfold is not passivity. It is a more patient form of attention. It looks like waiting to see whether someone follows through before reshaping our life around their commitment. It looks like sitting with a hard team question for another day instead of grabbing the first answer. It looks like noticing that the door we keep trying to pry open may not be the door we actually want to walk through.

At its best, letting it unfold protects us from confusing urgency with importance, anxiety with intuition, or control with care. It lets the situation tell us something we did not already know — about other people, about timing, and sometimes about ourselves.

## Letting it unfold, overused

When letting it unfold becomes the only move we have, it can quietly turn into avoidance. We may wait for a sign so clear it never arrives. We may call something patience when what is actually happening is a refusal to choose. Important conversations do not happen. Applications do not get sent. Boundaries do not get drawn. The shape of our life gets determined by other people's decisions, because we kept not making one of our own.

There is a difference between letting reality participate and asking reality to do our part for us.

## Questions to consider

- Which side of this polarity comes more easily to you? Does that change across work, friendship, family, or romantic life?
- Is there something in your life right now where you are doing most of the moving? What might reveal itself if you paused?
- Is there something you have been waiting on, hoping it will resolve on its own, where the next step is actually yours to take?
- Where might you be calling something "trust" that is closer to fear of choosing?
- Where might you be calling something "commitment" that is closer to control?
- What is yours to move forward right now? And what needs to be allowed to reveal itself first?
